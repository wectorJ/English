Game development is an exciting process that combines creativity and technical skills, resulting in the creation of fascinating worlds and unique gaming experiences. Every video game, be it an exciting action game, an exciting role-playing game, or an exciting puzzle game, starts with an idea, turns into a detailed project, and finally becomes a reality for millions of players around the world. From an idea to a finished game - let's dive into the world of creating virtual adventures and learn how games are born!





Planning





Before starting to develop your game, you need to answer several fundamental questions:

  • - What genre will your game be?
  • - Will it be 2D or 3D?
  • - What artistic style will you use?
  • - What gameplay mechanics do you want to include?
  • - Who is the hero, and who is the villain?
  • - What game engine will you use?
Ideas will be regularly refined and changed during the planning stage.




Proof of concept





After answering the big questions, you need to create evidence of the concept. Concept confirmation should answer such questions as:
Do you have all the necessary skills to create the game, or will you need to hire a team?

How much will it cost to create this game?

How will you get the necessary funds?

How much time will it take to create this game?

How will you monetize your game?
If you need to hire a team, how many people do you need? How big will the team be, and what roles will you need to fulfill?




Pre-production





 When the game concept is formed and the budget is approved, specialists begin preparing for production. This is where writers, artists, animators, programmers, engineers, and other important departments build plans for technical implementation and interaction.
The result of pre-production is ready sketches of characters, game environment and interface, control schemes, and other in-game elements. This allows you to see how they fit into a unified virtual world, how they feel as players, and how they interact with each other.




Production





Production is the longest and most important stage of game development. This is where the lion's share of your time and money will be spent. Production is where the fun is, but it's also where your ideas undergo the toughest testing, and tough decisions need to be made.

When games are in production:

  • - Developers and designers create the world of your game, creating and coding
  • - Models of main characters and NPCs are designed, rendered, and animated
  • - Voice actors record scripts and dialogue lines to find the perfect tone
  • - Sound designers create soundtracks and game effects
  • - Writers carefully write scripts and perform all minor copywriting tasks




Testing





Testing phases are a huge and exhaustive part of game production. At this stage, you need to check every single aspect of your game to identify any errors, glitches, exploits, or software blocks that may have slipped through.





Pre-launch





Preparation for launch is where your marketing kicks in. By now, you should have a stable beta version of your game ready for presentation.

This is one of the most exciting and nerve-wracking stages of the game development process - you are finally ready to showcase all your hard work, but you can't know how it will be received.





Launch





It’s time. Your game’s release day is just around the corner. Now’s the time to give your game its final coat of Poland polish.

The launch stage is your opportunity to:

  • — Squash any remaining bugs. Studios usually list all known bugs in priority order, starting with the game-breakers that affect stability and performance, down to the minor, cosmetic issues
  • — Implement simple quality of life improvements
  • — Add the final artistic touches to models and environments




Post launch





Congratulations - you’ve completed the game development process and made your own game! Soaring sales figures and swathes of positive reviews are sure to follow. There’s still a little bit of work to be done in the post-launch phase, though:

Downloadable content (DLC). Looking to revive some of that content you had to cut to meet your deadlines? DLC not only gives you that opportunity, but also helps you keep your game relevant with an injection of new content

Free content updates. The one thing players like more than paid DLC is free DLC

Game-balancing patches. They allow you to fully enjoy the game
Bug fixes. If you didn’t get the time to kill every minor bug before launch, or your players have discovered a few new ones, patch them out and tidy up the loose ends
